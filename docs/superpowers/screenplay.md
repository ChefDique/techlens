TECHLENS

Written by Richard Adair

SETTING: A 1950s-era automobile repair shop. Vintage aesthetic —
pin-up tool calendars, metal toolboxes, fluorescent tube lighting,
oil-stained concrete floors. But the technology is modern. Hydraulic
lifts. Touchscreen computers. Smartphones. The world looks old.
The tools are new. This is a shop caught between two centuries.

SCENE 1 — THE FUMBLE

INT. AUTO REPAIR SHOP — DAY

A pair of hands reach for a smartphone sitting on the fender of a
car with its hood up. The hands are covered in dark grease. Every
knuckle is black. The fingernails are lined with grime.

The phone slips. The technician fumbles it. It taps back and forth
between his greasy palms like a wet bar of soap. He catches it.
Presses the camera button. The phone takes a picture of the ceiling.

The technician — a weathered man in his fifties wearing a grey work
shirt with an oval name patch — tilts his head back. His reading
glasses sit low on the bridge of his nose. He holds the phone at
arm's length and squints at the screen, trying to find the button
to attach the photo to the repair order.

His greasy fingertip presses the screen. Nothing happens. He presses
again. The screen glitches. It won't register his touch. The grease
on his finger makes the screen think it's wet. He taps harder.
Nothing. He taps again. Nothing.

He throws the phone onto his tool cart.

SCENE 2 — THE GRAVEYARD

INT. AUTO REPAIR SHOP — COMPUTER STATION — CONTINUOUS

The technician walks toward a computer workstation at the back of
his bay. The camera follows behind him, over his shoulder.

He passes a wall of stacked paperback service manuals. Hundreds of
them. They are covered in a thin layer of shop dust. The spines are
cracked. The pages are yellowed. Nobody has touched most of them in
years.

He sits down at the computer. The screen shows a cluttered dealer
management system interface with tiny fonts and too many open tabs.
He clicks through screens. He scrolls through a PDF. He is looking
for a specific technical service bulletin. He cannot find it.

He mumbles curse words under his breath. They are inaudible but his
frustration is visible. His jaw is tight. His clicks get harder.

SCENE 3 — THE ARGUMENT

INT. AUTO REPAIR SHOP — TECHNICIAN BAY — CONTINUOUS

A service writer storms into the technician's bay from across the
shop. He is wearing a button-up shirt with a dealership logo on the
chest. He is holding a printed repair order. He is visibly
frustrated. He is being dramatic about it.

                    SERVICE WRITER
          What in the world does this even say?
          And what does this mean? Look at this —
          does that say milliamp? What the heck
          is an ohm?!

He shoves the repair order toward the technician's face. The
handwriting on it is barely legible.

The technician spins around in his chair.

                    OLD TECH
          You don't understand. First of all, I
          hate typing. And learning how to
          translate my sophisticated language
          into human-speak is your job. First I
          gotta log into this computer, then I
          gotta log into that system, then I
          gotta log into this other thing, then
          I gotta click on this in order to get
          to that — thirty minutes later I
          haven't even started diagnosing the
          car, and then you walk up to me saying
          my notes aren't good enough —

                    SERVICE WRITER
          Because they're NOT —

                    OLD TECH
          — that is not my problem!

They continue arguing. The camera slowly pulls back. Their voices
fade into the ambient noise of the shop. Air tools. A radio playing
somewhere. The hum of a compressor.

On screen, text fades in over the wide shot:

          "Every shop. Every day. Every car."

SCENE 4 — MEET RICHARD

INT. AUTO REPAIR SHOP — OPPOSITE SIDE — CONTINUOUS

The camera pans across the shop floor to a bay on the opposite
side. The energy here is different. It is cleaner. More organized.
The argument is still barely audible in the background, then fades
to nothing.

A series of quick shots reveal the workstation: an iPad propped on
a toolbox with Google NotebookLM open on screen. An iPhone mounted
to a magnetic holder on the lift post. A MacBook sitting on a
rolling cart with Gemini open in the browser.

                    CO-WORKER (O.S.)
          Yo Richard!

A young man stands up from behind the rear quarter panel of a
Subaru Outback. This is RICHARD ADAIR. Early twenties. Sharp eyes.
He is wearing a 1950s-style mechanic jumpsuit, clean enough to
suggest he cares about how he presents himself.

On his face he is wearing a pair of protective safety glasses.
Electrical-taped to the side of the frames is a small webcam. It
looks ridiculous. He looks like a mad scientist who works on cars.

He stands there for a beat. He knows how he looks.

                    CO-WORKER (O.S.)
          Do you know anything about that CVT
          problem and the new TSB that just came
          out? Thing is so confusing.

Richard doesn't hesitate.

                    RICHARD
          No I don't. But I got a buddy who does.

SCENE 5 — YO GEMMY

INT. AUTO REPAIR SHOP — RICHARD'S BAY — CONTINUOUS

Richard turns toward the MacBook on the rolling cart and calls out
like he is talking to someone across the room.

                    RICHARD
          Yo Gemmy!

The screen on the MacBook comes alive. An animated TechLens logo
appears with a pulsing audio waveform. It reacts to sound. It
feels alive. Like a presence in the room.

                    GEMMY (V.O.)
          Yes Richard, I'm here.

                    RICHARD
          I've got a, uhh — what did you say the
          year and model was?

A small motion graphic pops up on screen — the TechLens logo with
a chat bubble that reads:

          "Natural conversational flow...
          including interruptions."

The co-worker shouts from across the shop.

                    CO-WORKER (O.S.)
          Twenty-three Outback!

                    RICHARD
          2023 Outback, CVT issues — is there
          some new TSB?

                    GEMMY (V.O.)
          Yep. TSB 02-157-22R addresses customer
          concerns of transmission slippage and
          includes a lengthy diagnosis tree —
          thirty-five pages. You ready to begin?

                    RICHARD
          No. I need to take a leak.

A beat. Silence.

                    GEMMY (V.O.)
          Would you like the diagnosis flow chart
          for leak diagnosis?

A motion graphic pops up on screen — TechLens logo with a chat
bubble:

          "Context-aware. Even when you wish
          it wasn't."

SCENE 6 — UNDER THE CAR

INT. AUTO REPAIR SHOP — UNDER A LIFT — LATER

Text appears on screen:

          "10 MINUTES LATER"

Two pairs of legs are visible standing under a Subaru Outback on a
hydraulic lift. Richard and the co-worker are looking up at the
underside of the vehicle. They are examining the CVT transmission
area.

                    CO-WORKER
          I can't tell if this is the right part
          or if I'm just using the wrong tool...

Richard reaches up to his ridiculous eyewear rig and clicks on a
small flashlight mounted next to the webcam. The light illuminates
the underside of the transmission.

                    RICHARD
          Yo Gemmy, look at this!

A motion graphic appears on screen — TechLens logo with a chat
bubble:

          "Trigger words activate audio and
          video. Gemmy's there only when
          you want."

The screen splits. On the left side is what the webcam sees — the
CVT from below, illuminated by the flashlight, bolts and lines and
metal. On the right side is Gemmy's waveform pulsing as it responds.
A subtle overlay identifies the component being viewed.

                    GEMMY (V.O.)
          I can see that you're looking at the
          CVT. It looks like you have the correct
          location, but you need the specialized
          tool in order to complete the repair.

                    RICHARD
          Well, that's a bust.

                    CO-WORKER
          Yeah, but at least we weren't wasting
          a bunch of time trying to figure out
          if we had the right tool. Or if we even
          have the tool.

                    GEMMY (V.O.)
          That's very true. But if you show me
          your special tools, I could possibly
          tell you which one it is — if you do
          have it.

                    RICHARD
          No, that'll be fine. It's time to go
          home.

SCENE 7 — THE WRAP-UP

INT. AUTO REPAIR SHOP — RICHARD'S BAY — CONTINUOUS

Richard and the co-worker walk out from under the lift. They wipe
their hands on shop rags. The day is winding down. Other bays are
going dark.

                    GEMMY (V.O.)
          Alright. I'm going to summarize the
          specific steps that we took today and
          note the important details — especially
          the one about not having the tool to
          finish the job.

On the MacBook screen, three documents animate into view, side by
side:

The first is labeled TECH NOTES. It is structured and clinical.
Bullet points. Part numbers. Diagnostic codes.

The second is labeled CUSTOMER SUMMARY. It is written in plain
English. Short paragraphs. No jargon. A customer could read this
and understand what happened to their car.

The third is labeled ESCALATION BRIEF. It is formal and detailed.
Measurements. DTCs. Every diagnostic step documented. This is what
a manufacturer field engineer needs to see.

A motion graphic appears — TechLens logo with text:

          "One session. Three outputs. Tech
          notes. Customer summary. Escalation
          brief."

                    GEMMY (V.O.)
          I've added it to the repair order.
          Don't forget — there are two more
          complaint lines that need to be
          addressed tomorrow. Get some rest!

Richard pulls off his goofy eyewear rig. He smiles. He grabs his
bag and walks toward the shop exit. The co-worker glances at the
three documents on the screen. He nods. He gets it.

Quick cut to: the old technician from Scene 1. He is still at his
computer. He is still typing with one greasy finger. Hunt and peck.
One letter at a time.

SCENE 8 — THIS IS REAL

The screen cuts to black for one beat. When the image returns, the
1950s palette is gone. We are looking at a real screen. The actual
TechLens application.

                    RICHARD (V.O.)
          TechLens is real. Here's the actual
          product.

Screen recording: the TechLens web app is running. A voice session
starts. A technician's voice goes in. The agent responds in real
time. The camera feed is active. Tool calls are happening.

                    RICHARD (V.O.)
          One voice session replaces thirty
          minutes of typing, three logins, and
          a stack of manuals.

An architecture diagram fills the screen. React flows to Cloud Run,
which connects to Gemini Live API and Firestore. Google Cloud
service logos are visible.

                    RICHARD (V.O.)
          Built on Google's Gemini Live API and
          Agent Development Kit. Deployed on
          Cloud Run with Firestore.

Screen recording: the three output documents generate from a real
session. Tech notes. Customer summary. Escalation brief. They
appear on screen exactly as promised.

                    RICHARD (V.O.)
          Every session automatically generates
          tech notes, a customer summary, and an
          escalation brief.

Richard speaks directly to the camera. Or his voice plays over a
simple text card. Either way, this is him. Not the character. The
real person.

                    RICHARD (V.O.)
          I spent years as the translator between
          techs, writers, customers, and
          manufacturer reps. TechLens does that
          job automatically.

SCENE 9 — WHAT'S NEXT

A quick montage of concept images: real smart glasses — not a

repair orders flowing in and out. Logos of multiple manufacturers,
not just Subaru.

                    RICHARD (V.O.)
          Next — real smart glasses integration,
          DMS connectivity, and every
          manufacturer. Not just Subaru.

The screen fades to the TechLens logo centered on a clean
background.
